Overview

I am currently studying how changes in behavioral context affect the neural population coding of sounds in auditory cortex. To do this, I use multi-channel laminar electrodes to record neural population activity from the auditory cortex of awake, behaving animals while presenting sound stimuli. I use computational analyses to determine how the neural representation of sound changes as a function of behavioral context.
With this work, I hope to gain fundamental insights about neural processing that will ultimately contribute to the improvement of engineered systems for sensory signal processing.

Projects

Population coding of natural stimuli

Shared trial to trial response variability amongst populations of neurons is ubiquitous in cortex. The structure of this correlated activity places constraints on neural coding accuracy and has been shown to depend on behavioral states such as task-engagement, selective attention, and arousal. The majority of existing work has focused on how changes in correlated activity affect single trial coding of simple, parametric stimuli. I am interested in extending this work to understand how changes in behavioral state affect the coding of naturalistic stimuli.

Dissociation of task-engagement and arousal

Both task-engagement and arousal have been shown to modulate neural activity in similar ways, with most reports describing increased evoked response magnitude and decreased trial to trial variability (discussed above). Task-engagement is almost always accompanied by a concomitant increase in global arousal. Therefore, it remains unknown if goal-directed changes in behavioral state, like task-engagement, engage the same, or different, neural mechanisms as arousal. A major part of my thesis work is focused on this question.
